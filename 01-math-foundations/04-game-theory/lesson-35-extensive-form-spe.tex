%% SECTION 1: EXTENSIVE-FORM GAMES

\section{Extensive-Form Games}
\label{sec:extensive}

Normal-form games capture simultaneous interaction. Many strategic
situations, however, unfold sequentially: players observe previous moves
before choosing their own. The extensive form models such sequential
structure via game trees.

\subsection{Game Trees and Information Sets}
\label{subsec:game_trees}

\begin{definition}[Extensive-Form Game]
\label{def:extensive}
An \emph{extensive-form game} is a tuple
$\Gamma = (N, H, P, (\mathcal{I}_i)_{i \in N}, u)$ where:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\roman*)]
    \item $N = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ is the set of players. We may include a
    ``chance'' player~$0$ (nature).
    \item $H$ is a set of \emph{histories} (finite sequences of actions),
    including the empty sequence $\emptyset$ (the root). $H$ is prefix-closed:
    if $(a_1, \ldots, a_k) \in H$ then $(a_1, \ldots, a_l) \in H$ for all
    $l \leq k$. A history $h \in H$ is \emph{terminal} if there is no action
    $a$ with $(h, a) \in H$. The set of terminal histories is $Z \subseteq H$.
    \item $P : H \setminus Z \to N \cup \{0\}$ is the \emph{player function},
    assigning to each non-terminal history the player who acts there.
    \item For each $i \in N$, $\mathcal{I}_i$ is a partition of
    $\{h \in H \setminus Z : P(h) = i\}$ into \emph{information sets}. If
    $h, h'$ belong to the same information set $I \in \mathcal{I}_i$, then
    the available actions satisfy $A(h) = A(h')$, denoted $A(I)$.
    \item $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_n) : Z \to \R^n$ assigns payoffs to terminal
    histories.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Perfect Information]
\label{def:perfect_info}
An extensive-form game has \emph{perfect information} if every information
set is a singleton: $|I| = 1$ for all $I \in \mathcal{I}_i$, all $i \in N$.
At each decision point, the acting player knows the complete history of play.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Perfect Recall]
\label{def:perfect_recall}
Player $i$ has \emph{perfect recall} if for any two histories $h, h'$ in the
same information set $I \in \mathcal{I}_i$, the sequence of player~$i$'s
information sets and actions along the path from the root to $h$ equals the
corresponding sequence along the path to $h'$. Informally, no player forgets
their own past actions or past information.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Entry Game]
\label{ex:entry}
An incumbent (player~2) faces a potential entrant (player~1). The entrant
first decides to \emph{Enter} ($E$) or \emph{Stay Out} ($O$). If the
entrant enters, the incumbent decides to \emph{Fight} ($F$) or
\emph{Accommodate} ($A$). Payoffs:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $O$: $(0, 2)$.
    \item $(E, F)$: $(-1, -1)$.
    \item $(E, A)$: $(1, 1)$.
\end{itemize}
This is a perfect-information game: all information sets are singletons.
The histories are $H = \{\emptyset, O, E, (E,F), (E,A)\}$, with
$Z = \{O, (E,F), (E,A)\}$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Simultaneous Move as Imperfect Information]
\label{ex:simultaneous}
A simultaneous $2 \times 2$ game can be represented in extensive form as
follows: player~1 moves first (choosing $T$ or $B$), then player~2 moves
without observing player~1's choice. Player~2's two decision nodes
(after $T$ and after $B$) belong to a single information set. This shows
that \emph{simultaneous} games are a special case of extensive-form games
with imperfect information.
\end{example}

\subsection{Strategies in Extensive-Form Games}
\label{subsec:strategies}

\begin{definition}[Pure Strategy in Extensive Form]
\label{def:strategy_extensive}
A \emph{pure strategy} for player $i$ in an extensive-form game is a function
$s_i : \mathcal{I}_i \to \bigcup_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i} A(I)$ satisfying
$s_i(I) \in A(I)$ for each $I \in \mathcal{I}_i$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Complete Contingent Plans]
\label{rem:contingent}
A strategy specifies an action at \emph{every} information set, including
those unreachable given earlier choices. In the Entry Game
(Example~\ref{ex:entry}), player~2 has two pure strategies ($F$ and $A$)
even though their decision node is reached only if player~1 enters. This
apparent redundancy is essential for defining Nash equilibrium and subgame
perfection.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Behavioral Strategy]
\label{def:behavioral}
A \emph{behavioral strategy} for player $i$ is a function
$b_i : \mathcal{I}_i \to \bigcup_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i} \Delta(A(I))$
satisfying $b_i(I) \in \Delta(A(I))$. That is, $b_i$ assigns a probability
distribution over actions at each information set, with independent
randomization across information sets.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Normal-Form Representation]
\label{prop:normal_form_rep}
Every extensive-form game has a corresponding normal-form game: the strategy
sets are as in Definition~\ref{def:strategy_extensive}, and the payoff
$u_i(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ is determined by following the unique path through
the tree dictated by the strategy profile (with expectations over chance moves).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Given a strategy profile $(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$, define the path from the root
inductively. At each non-terminal history $h$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item If $P(h) = i \in N$, the next action is $s_i(I)$ where $I$ is the
    information set containing $h$.
    \item If $P(h) = 0$ (chance), the next action is drawn from the
    prescribed chance distribution.
\end{itemize}
Since the game tree is finite, this terminates at a terminal history $z \in Z$.
Define $u_i(s_1, \ldots, s_n) = \E[u_i(z)]$, where the expectation is over
chance moves. The tuple $(N, (S_i), (u_i))$ is a well-defined normal-form
game.
\end{proof}

%% SECTION 2: KUHN'S THEOREM

\section{Kuhn's Theorem}
\label{sec:kuhn}

The relationship between mixed strategies (distributions over pure strategies)
and behavioral strategies (local randomization at each information set) is
fundamental. In general, behavioral strategies are a strict subset of what
mixed strategies can achieve. Kuhn's theorem identifies the exact condition
under which they coincide.

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{theorem}[Kuhn, 1953]
\label{thm:kuhn}
In a finite extensive-form game with perfect recall, every mixed strategy of
player $i$ has a \emph{realization-equivalent} behavioral strategy: a
behavioral strategy inducing the same probability distribution over terminal
histories for every strategy profile of the opponents. Conversely, every
behavioral strategy has a realization-equivalent mixed strategy.
\end{theorem}
\end{keyresult}

\begin{proof}
We prove both directions.

\textbf{Direction 1: Mixed to behavioral.}
Let $\sigma_i$ be a mixed strategy (a distribution over pure strategies
$S_i$) for player $i$ in a game with perfect recall. We construct a
behavioral strategy $b_i$ and show realization equivalence.

For each information set $I \in \mathcal{I}_i$, define the \emph{reach
probability} $\rho_i(I) = \sigma_i(\{s_i \in S_i : s_i \text{ reaches } I\})$,
where ``$s_i$ reaches $I$'' means there exists some opponent profile under
which the path of play passes through a history in $I$ when player $i$
follows $s_i$. Under perfect recall, whether $s_i$ reaches $I$ depends only
on $s_i$'s actions at player $i$'s information sets on the path to $I$
(not on actions at information sets after or disjoint from $I$).

For each action $a \in A(I)$, define:
\[
    b_i(a | I) = \begin{cases}
    \displaystyle\frac{\sigma_i(\{s_i : s_i(I) = a \text{ and }
    s_i \text{ reaches } I\})}{\rho_i(I)}
    & \text{if } \rho_i(I) > 0, \\[6pt]
    \displaystyle\frac{1}{|A(I)|} & \text{if } \rho_i(I) = 0.
    \end{cases}
\]
When $\rho_i(I) > 0$, the numerator sums probabilities of pure strategies
that both reach $I$ and play $a$ there, so $b_i(\cdot | I)$ is a well-defined
distribution.

We verify realization equivalence. The \emph{realization probability} of a
terminal history $z$ under $\sigma_i$ (with opponents playing $s_{-i}$) is:
\[
    \Prob_{\sigma_i}(z \mid s_{-i})
    = \sigma_i\big(\{s_i : (s_i, s_{-i}) \text{ leads to } z\}\big).
\]
Under the behavioral strategy $b_i$, the probability of the same terminal
history is:
\[
    \Prob_{b_i}(z \mid s_{-i})
    = \prod_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i^z} b_i(a_I^z \mid I),
\]
where $\mathcal{I}_i^z$ is the set of player $i$'s information sets on the
path to $z$, and $a_I^z$ is the action taken at $I$ on this path.

The key step uses perfect recall. Under perfect recall, the information sets
$\mathcal{I}_i^z$ are linearly ordered along the path, and the set of pure
strategies reaching $z$ factors as:
\[
    \{s_i : (s_i, s_{-i}) \text{ leads to } z\}
    = \bigcap_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i^z} \{s_i : s_i(I) = a_I^z
    \text{ and } s_i \text{ reaches } I\}.
\]
By perfect recall, knowing that $s_i$ reaches $I$ and plays $a_I^z$ there
determines that $s_i$ reaches the next information set $I'$ on the path.
This gives a telescoping product:
\begin{align*}
    \Prob_{\sigma_i}(z \mid s_{-i})
    &= \sigma_i\bigg(\bigcap_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i^z}
    \{s_i : s_i(I) = a_I^z, s_i \text{ reaches } I\}\bigg) \\
    &= \prod_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i^z}
    \frac{\sigma_i(\{s_i : s_i(I) = a_I^z, s_i \text{ reaches } I\})}
    {\sigma_i(\{s_i : s_i \text{ reaches } I\})} \\
    &= \prod_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i^z} b_i(a_I^z \mid I)
    = \Prob_{b_i}(z \mid s_{-i}).
\end{align*}
The second equality uses the chain rule and the perfect recall structure: the
conditional probability of playing $a_I^z$ at $I$ given reaching $I$ is
independent of what happens at other information sets not on the path
between the previous information set and $I$.

\textbf{Direction 2: Behavioral to mixed.}
Given a behavioral strategy $b_i$, define a mixed strategy by
$\sigma_i(s_i) = \prod_{I \in \mathcal{I}_i} b_i(s_i(I) | I)$.
This treats each information set as an independent randomization device.
By construction, the probability that the path passes through any sequence
of information-set/action pairs is the same product under both $\sigma_i$
and $b_i$, establishing realization equivalence.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Computational Significance]
\label{rem:kuhn_computational}
A behavioral strategy requires $O(\sum_I |A(I)|)$ parameters (one
distribution per information set), while a mixed strategy over pure strategies
may require exponentially many parameters (one probability per complete
contingent plan). Kuhn's theorem says we lose nothing by working with the
compact behavioral representation.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Failure Without Perfect Recall]
\label{prop:no_perfect_recall}
In games without perfect recall, mixed and behavioral strategies may yield
different sets of reachable outcome distributions.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Consider a one-player game where the player moves twice but forgets their
first action. Specifically: the player first chooses $L$ or $R$, then (in
a single information set containing both post-$L$ and post-$R$ nodes)
chooses $A$ or $B$. Payoffs: $(L,A) \mapsto 1$, $(L,B) \mapsto 0$,
$(R,A) \mapsto 0$, $(R,B) \mapsto 1$.

A behavioral strategy specifies $p = \Prob(L)$ at the first node and
$q = \Prob(A)$ at the second information set. The expected payoff is
$pq + (1-p)(1-q) = 1 - p - q + 2pq$, which has maximum $1/2$ (at
$p = q = 1/2$).

The mixed strategy playing $(L,A)$ with probability $1/2$ and $(R,B)$ with
probability $1/2$ achieves expected payoff $1/2 \cdot 1 + 1/2 \cdot 1 = 1$.
This correlates the two moves, which no behavioral strategy can do, since
behavioral strategies randomize independently at each information set.
\end{proof}

\begin{rlconnection}
A behavioral strategy is precisely an RL policy: it maps information states
(information sets) to distributions over actions. Kuhn's theorem justifies
the standard RL practice of parameterizing policies as functions
$\pi(a|s)$ rather than distributions over complete plans. The perfect recall
condition corresponds to the Markov property: the agent's policy depends
only on the current information state, not on the randomization device
used to reach it.
\end{rlconnection}

%% SECTION 3: SUBGAME PERFECT EQUILIBRIUM

\section{Subgame Perfect Equilibrium}
\label{sec:spe}

\subsection{Non-Credible Threats}
\label{subsec:noncredible}

\begin{example}[Non-Credible Threat in the Entry Game]
\label{ex:noncredible}
In the Entry Game (Example~\ref{ex:entry}), consider the strategy profile
$(O, F)$: player~1 stays out, player~2 threatens to fight. Payoffs: $(0,2)$.

Is this a NE? Player~1's deviation to $E$ yields $-1$ (since player~2
plays $F$), so player~1 has no incentive to deviate. Player~2's information
set is never reached, so any strategy gives the same outcome. Hence $(O,F)$
is a NE. However, if player~1 did enter, player~2 would prefer to
accommodate (payoff $1$) over fighting (payoff $-1$). The threat to fight
is \emph{non-credible}: it would not be carried out if the relevant decision
point were actually reached. Subgame perfection eliminates such equilibria.
\end{example}

\subsection{Subgames}
\label{subsec:subgame_def}

\begin{definition}[Subgame]
\label{def:subgame}
A \emph{subgame} of $\Gamma$ beginning at history $h \in H \setminus Z$ is
the restriction of $\Gamma$ to histories extending $h$, provided:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\roman*)]
    \item $\{h\}$ is a singleton information set for player $P(h)$.
    \item Every information set containing a successor of $h$ is entirely
    contained within the subtree rooted at $h$.
\end{enumerate}
The subgame inherits the player function, information partition (restricted
to the subtree), and payoffs from $\Gamma$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:subgame_count}
In a game with perfect information, every non-terminal history begins a
subgame (since all information sets are singletons and condition (ii) is
automatic). In games with imperfect information, subgames may be rare. The
entire game (rooted at $\emptyset$) is always a subgame.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Subgame Perfect Equilibrium]
\label{def:spe}
A strategy profile $s^*$ is a \emph{subgame perfect equilibrium} (SPE) if
its restriction to every subgame is a Nash equilibrium of that subgame.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[SPE Refines NE]
\label{prop:spe_refines}
Every subgame perfect equilibrium is a Nash equilibrium.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The entire game is a subgame (beginning at the root $\emptyset$). If $s^*$
is an SPE, then by definition it is a NE of every subgame, including the
entire game.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[SPE in the Entry Game]
\label{ex:spe_entry}
The Entry Game has two subgames: the whole game (rooted at $\emptyset$) and
the subgame starting after $E$ (where player~2 chooses $F$ or $A$).

In the subgame after $E$: player~2 chooses $A$ (payoff $1 > -1$). The unique
NE of this subgame is $A$.

In the full game: player~1 anticipates player~2 accommodating, so entry gives
payoff $1 > 0$ (staying out). Hence player~1 enters.

The unique SPE is $(E, A)$ with payoffs $(1, 1)$. The NE $(O, F)$ is
eliminated because $F$ is not a NE in the post-entry subgame.
\end{example}

\subsection{Backward Induction}
\label{subsec:backward_induction}

\begin{definition}[Backward Induction]
\label{def:backward_induction}
For a finite game with perfect information, \emph{backward induction}
proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Identify all \emph{penultimate nodes}: non-terminal histories $h$
    such that all successors $(h, a)$ are terminal. At each such $h$, player
    $P(h)$ chooses the action maximizing their payoff:
    $a^*(h) = \argmax_{a \in A(h)} u_{P(h)}(h, a)$.
    \item Replace each penultimate node $h$ with a terminal node having payoff
    $u(h, a^*(h))$.
    \item Repeat until the root is reached.
\end{enumerate}
The resulting strategy profile assigns an action at every node of the
original tree.
\end{definition}

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{theorem}[Backward Induction Yields SPE]
\label{thm:bi_spe}
In a finite game with perfect information and no ties (the payoff function
is injective when restricted to any player's choices at any node), backward
induction produces the unique subgame perfect equilibrium.
\end{theorem}
\end{keyresult}

\begin{proof}
We proceed by strong induction on the depth $d$ of the game tree (the
maximum number of actions in any history).

\textbf{Base case ($d = 1$):} The game consists of a single player choosing
at the root, with all successors terminal. Backward induction selects the
payoff-maximizing action, which is unique (no ties). This is trivially the
unique SPE.

\textbf{Inductive step:} Assume the result holds for all games of depth $< d$.
Consider a game $\Gamma$ of depth $d$ with perfect information and no ties.
At the root, player $P(\emptyset)$ has actions $A(\emptyset) = \{a_1, \ldots, a_l\}$.
Each action $a_j$ leads to a subgame $\Gamma_{a_j}$ of depth $< d$. By the
inductive hypothesis, backward induction produces the unique SPE $s^*_{a_j}$
of $\Gamma_{a_j}$, with payoff vector $v(a_j) \in \R^n$.

Backward induction assigns to the root the action
$a^* = \argmax_{a_j} v_{P(\emptyset)}(a_j)$, which is unique since there
are no ties. Define $s^*$ as: play $a^*$ at the root, and follow $s^*_{a_j}$
in each subgame $\Gamma_{a_j}$.

\emph{$s^*$ is an SPE:} In each subgame $\Gamma_{a_j}$, the restriction of
$s^*$ is $s^*_{a_j}$, an SPE by hypothesis. In the full game: the root
player chooses $a^*$ optimally given the unique SPE continuations, and no
other player moves at the root. Hence no player has a profitable deviation
in any subgame.

\emph{Uniqueness:} Any SPE $\hat{s}$ must restrict to the unique SPE in each
$\Gamma_{a_j}$ (by the inductive hypothesis), so the root player faces the
same payoffs $v(a_j)$ and, with no ties, must choose $a^*$. Hence
$\hat{s} = s^*$.
\end{proof}

%% SECTION 4: ZERMELO'S THEOREM

\section{Zermelo's Theorem}
\label{sec:zermelo}

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{theorem}[Zermelo, 1913]
\label{thm:zermelo}
Every finite extensive-form game with perfect information has a pure strategy
subgame perfect equilibrium. If the game also has no ties (each player has
distinct payoffs at all terminal nodes reachable from any decision node), the
SPE is unique.
\end{theorem}
\end{keyresult}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Existence.} We prove by strong induction on $|H \setminus Z|$, the
number of non-terminal histories.

\emph{Base case ($|H \setminus Z| = 1$):} The game has a single decision
node (the root) with terminal successors. The acting player has at least
one payoff-maximizing action (finite, non-empty action set). Any such action
constitutes a pure strategy SPE.

\emph{Inductive step:} Let $\Gamma$ have $|H \setminus Z| = k > 1$. The
root player $P(\emptyset)$ has actions $a_1, \ldots, a_l$. Each $a_j$ leads
to a subgame $\Gamma_{a_j}$ with strictly fewer non-terminal histories. By
the inductive hypothesis, each $\Gamma_{a_j}$ has a pure strategy SPE
$s^*_{a_j}$ with payoff vector $v(a_j)$.

Define $s^*$: at the root, choose
$a^* \in \argmax_{a_j} v_{P(\emptyset)}(a_j)$ (non-empty since $l$ is
finite); in each subgame $\Gamma_{a_j}$, follow $s^*_{a_j}$.

We verify $s^*$ is an SPE. For any subgame not containing the root, the
restriction of $s^*$ is $s^*_{a_j}$ for the appropriate $j$, which is an SPE
by hypothesis. For the entire game: the root player optimizes given the SPE
continuations (by construction), and no other player moves at the root. Hence
$s^*$ is an SPE.

\textbf{Uniqueness under no ties.} By Theorem~\ref{thm:bi_spe}, backward
induction produces the unique SPE when all choices have distinct payoffs.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Chess Has a Determined Outcome]
\label{cor:chess}
In chess (viewed as a finite game with perfect information---the 50-move
rule and threefold repetition ensure finiteness), exactly one of the
following holds: White has a winning strategy, Black has a winning strategy,
or both players can force a draw.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Chess is a two-player, finite, perfect-information game with three possible
outcomes: White wins, Black wins, or draw. By Zermelo's theorem, a pure
strategy SPE exists. In this SPE, the outcome is determined (it is one of
the three possibilities). The SPE strategy for the favored side (or both
sides in the draw case) guarantees this outcome regardless of the opponent's
play, since in any subgame the SPE prescribes optimal behavior.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Zermelo vs.\ Minimax]
\label{rem:zermelo_minimax}
For two-player zero-sum perfect-information games, Zermelo's theorem and the
minimax theorem (Lesson~34, Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax}) are related but
distinct. Zermelo guarantees a pure SPE in the extensive form. The minimax
theorem guarantees saddle-point equality in the normal form (possibly
requiring mixed strategies in general). For finite perfect-information
zero-sum games, both results apply, and the backward induction value equals
the minimax value.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Tic-Tac-Toe]
\label{ex:tictactoe}
Tic-tac-toe is a finite, two-player, perfect-information game with three
outcomes (X wins, O wins, draw). By Zermelo's theorem, the game has a
determined outcome. It is known (by exhaustive computation of the game tree,
which has fewer than $9! = 362880$ terminal nodes) that both players can
force a draw. The SPE consists of optimal strategies guaranteeing at least a
draw for each player.
\end{example}

%% SECTION 5: RL CONNECTIONS

\section{Connections to Reinforcement Learning}
\label{sec:rl_connections}

\begin{rlconnection}
\textbf{MDPs as single-player extensive-form games.} A finite-horizon MDP
with deterministic transitions is a single-player game with perfect
information. The ``player'' is the agent, the ``histories'' are sequences of
state-action pairs, and the ``payoff'' at a terminal history is the
cumulative reward. Backward induction is precisely dynamic programming: the
Bellman equation $V_t(s) = \max_a [r(s,a) + V_{t+1}(s')]$ computes the
optimal action at each state by working backward from the horizon.

With stochastic transitions, nature (player~0) acts between the agent's
moves, introducing chance nodes. The backward induction computation becomes
$V_t(s) = \max_a [r(s,a) + \E_{s'}[V_{t+1}(s')]]$, which is the standard
Bellman optimality equation.
\end{rlconnection}

\begin{rlconnection}
\textbf{Multi-agent backward induction.} In multi-agent settings (stochastic
games), backward induction at each state requires solving a game rather than
an optimization. This is the foundation of:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Minimax-Q} \citep{shoham2008}: backward induction with
    minimax stage games in zero-sum stochastic games.
    \item \textbf{AlphaGo/AlphaZero}: Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)
    approximately performs backward induction on enormous game trees by
    sampling trajectories and using neural network value estimates.
    \item \textbf{Subgame solving} in poker: computing SPE (or
    approximations) in subgames of imperfect-information games.
\end{itemize}
\end{rlconnection}

\section{Exercises}
\label{sec:exercises}

\begin{exercise}[Normal-Form Representation]
\label{ex:normal_form}
Consider the Entry Game (Example~\ref{ex:entry}).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
    \item List all pure strategies for each player.
    \item Write the normal-form (bimatrix) representation.
    \item Find all pure strategy NE of the normal form.
    \item Identify which NE are subgame perfect.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Three-Stage Backward Induction]
\label{ex:three_stage}
Player~1 chooses $A$ or $B$. If $A$: player~2 chooses $C$ or $D$.
If $B$: player~2 chooses $E$ or $F$. If $A \to D$: player~1 chooses
$G$ or $H$. Terminal payoffs (player~1, player~2):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $A \to C$: $(3,2)$, \quad $A \to D \to G$: $(4,1)$,
    \quad $A \to D \to H$: $(1,3)$.
    \item $B \to E$: $(2,4)$, \quad $B \to F$: $(5,0)$.
\end{itemize}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
    \item Apply backward induction step by step.
    \item Write the complete SPE strategy for each player.
    \item How many pure strategy NE does the normal form have? Which are
    subgame perfect?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Kuhn's Theorem Verification]
\label{ex:kuhn_verify}
In a two-player game, player~1 observes a private type $t \in \{H, L\}$
(each equally likely) and sends a message $m \in \{h, l\}$. Player~2
observes the message but not the type, and chooses $U$ or $D$.
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
    \item How many pure strategies does each player have?
    \item Verify that this game has perfect recall.
    \item Construct a behavioral strategy for player~1 that plays $h$ with
    probability $3/4$ when $t = H$ and $l$ with probability $1$ when $t = L$.
    Write the realization-equivalent mixed strategy over pure strategies.
    \item Explain why Kuhn's theorem applies and state its consequence.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Non-Credible Threats in a Richer Game]
\label{ex:noncredible_rich}
An entrant chooses $E$ or $O$. If $E$, the incumbent chooses $F$
(fight), $A$ (accommodate), or $P$ (price war). Payoffs:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $O$: $(0, 3)$, \quad $(E, F)$: $(-2, -1)$, \quad $(E, A)$: $(1, 1)$,
    \quad $(E, P)$: $(-3, -2)$.
\end{itemize}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
    \item Find all pure strategy NE of the normal-form representation.
    \item Identify which NE involve non-credible threats and explain why.
    \item Find the unique SPE.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Subgame Count]
\label{ex:subgame_count}
Consider a three-player game: player~1 chooses $L$ or $R$. If $L$:
players~2 and~3 move simultaneously (player~2 chooses $A$ or $B$, player~3
chooses $X$ or $Y$, neither observing the other's choice). If $R$: player~2
chooses $C$ or $D$ (with player~3 not moving).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
    \item Draw the game tree with information sets.
    \item How many subgames does this game have?
    \item Explain why the simultaneous move after $L$ does not generate
    additional subgames.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Zermelo and Nim]
\label{ex:nim}
In the game of Nim with a single pile of $n$ stones, two players alternate
removing $1$, $2$, or $3$ stones. The player who takes the last stone wins
(payoff $1$; the loser gets $0$).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
    \item Show this is a finite perfect-information game.
    \item Use backward induction to determine the winner for $n = 1, 2, 3, 4$.
    \item Prove by induction that player~1 wins if and only if $n$ is not
    divisible by $4$.
    \item Relate this to Zermelo's theorem.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[SPE in a Bargaining Game]
\label{ex:bargaining}
Two players divide a dollar. In round~1, player~1 proposes a split
$(x, 1-x)$ with $x \in \{0, 0.5, 1\}$. Player~2 accepts (game ends with
these payoffs) or rejects. If rejected, in round~2, player~2 proposes
$(y, 1-y)$ with $y \in \{0, 0.5, 1\}$, and player~1 accepts or rejects.
If rejected again, both get $0$.
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
    \item Model this as an extensive-form game and count the subgames.
    \item Apply backward induction to find the SPE.
    \item How does the SPE change if round~2 payoffs are discounted by
    $\delta \in (0,1)$?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Failure of SPE Refinement Under Imperfect Information]
\label{ex:spe_imperfect}
Construct a two-player extensive-form game with imperfect information that
has exactly one subgame (the entire game) and a NE in which some player's
strategy is not ``sequentially rational'' at a non-singleton information set.
Explain why SPE fails to refine NE in this case and what stronger solution
concept is needed.
\end{exercise}

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Summary.} This lesson developed: (1) the extensive-form
game model with histories, information sets, and the distinction between
perfect and imperfect information; (2) pure and behavioral strategies, with
Kuhn's theorem establishing their equivalence under perfect recall; (3)
subgame perfection as a refinement of Nash equilibrium that eliminates
non-credible threats; (4) backward induction as a constructive algorithm
that produces the unique SPE in perfect-information games without ties; and
(5) Zermelo's theorem guaranteeing SPE existence in all finite
perfect-information games. The connection to RL is direct: backward induction
is single-agent dynamic programming, and behavioral strategies are RL
policies.
